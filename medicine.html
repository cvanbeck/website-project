How can AI help in the medical world?

The Role of Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is slowly but surely becoming an essential part of healthcare. Thanks to AI-powered applications such as deep learning algorithms among other things being used to process medical data, we’re well on our way to providing experts in the field with much-needed perception, and improving patient experiences and overall health results.

Presently, only very specific settings in the field of medicine profit from the application of artificial intelligence, for example seizures, hypoglycemia, or the diagnosis of disease based on medical imaging. The pursuance of augmented medicine is highly anticipated by patients because it aims for a greater freedom and a more personalized treatment although it is met with great hostility from specialists which were not ready for such an adaptation of clinical practice.

This phenomena also brings about the need to validate these modern equipments with traditional clinical trials, discuss the educational improvement of the medical curriculum because of digital medicine along with ethical consideration of the current connected monitoring.

Machine Learning has made great advances in pharma and biotech efficiency.

Diagnosing Diseases

First of all great strides have been made in diagnosing diseases. Accurately diagnosing diseases takes years of medical training. Even then, diagnostics is generally a taxing, time-consuming procedure. In many fields, the demand for professionals far outweighs the accessible supply. This puts physicians under pressure and often delays life-saving patient diagnostics. Machine Learning, particularly Deep Learning algorithms, have lately made huge advances in automatically detecting diseases, making diagnostics cheaper and more accessible.

Machine Learning algorithms can master pattern detection similarly to the way doctors see them. A key distinction is that algorithms need a lot of concrete examples in order to learn. And these examples need to be neatly digitized as machines can’t read between the lines in textbooks.

So Machine Learning is particularly beneficial in areas where the diagnostic information a physician examines is already digitized. For example finding signs of diabetic retinopathy in eye images, categorizing skin lesions in skin images, discovering strokes or lung cancer based on CT scans.

Drug Development
Secondly Machine Learning is well on its way to develop drugs faster. Developing drugs is an expensive process. A lot of the analytical processes involved in drug development can be made more effective with Machine Learning. This has the promise of shaving off years of work and hundreds of millions in investments. AI has already been used favourably in all of the four main stages in drug development.
  • First stage: Spotting targets for intervention
  • Second stage: Recognizing drug candidates
  • Third stage: Accelerating clinical trials
  • Fourth stage: Finding Biomarkers for diagnosing the disease
Personalised Treatment

Thirdly personalizing treatment. Different patients respond to drugs and treatment schedules differently. Therefore personalized treatment has tremendous potential to prolong patients’ lifespans. But it’s very difficult to label which components should affect the choice of treatment.

Machine Learning can mechanize this complex statistical work and help ascertain which attributes implies that a patient will have a particular response to a particular treatment. So the algorithm can predict a patient’s probable response to a particular treatment. The system figures this out by cross-referencing comparable patients and associates their treatments and outcomes. The resulting outcome predictions makes it easier for physicians to plan out the right treatment program.

Gene Editing

Fourthly improving gene editing. Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR), is a huge step forward in the cability to edit DNA cost effectively and accurately just like a surgeon. This approach depends on short guide RNA (sgRNA) to target and edit a definitive region on the DNA but the guide RNA can fit numerous DNA locations which can result in inadvertent side effects known as off-target effects. The meticulous drafting of guide RNA with the least perilous side effects is a major hindrance in the application of the CRISPR system. Machine Learning models are tried and tested in the provision of the best results when it comes to forecasting the degree of both guide-target interactions and off-target effects for a given sgRNA. This can substantially speed up the augmentation of guide RNA for every region of human DNA.

Artificial Intelligence is already aiding us more adequately diagnose diseases, develop drugs, personalize treatments, and even edit genes. But this is only the beginning. The more we computerize and consolidate the medical data available to us, the more we can use AI to help us find beneficial patterns we can use in making detailed, cost-effective outcomes in complex analytical processes.
